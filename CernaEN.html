assertText//div[@id='sectionCollapse0']/pOne of the most spectacular National Parks in Romania, Domogled - Cerna Valley National Park stretches over three counties: Caras-Severin, Mehedinti and Gorj and is the largest park of its kind in the country. The geographic richness of the park includes unique caves, limestone peaks, thermal mineral springs, mountain accumulation lakes, subalpine meadows and more than 100 species of plants and animals, including many rare species. There are more than 30 mountain trails, beech forests, juniper fields above the tree line and canyons carved by “moody” streams, with high fluctuating water flows. On the 61,211 hectares, there are 11 natural reservations (Coronini - Bedina, Domogled Reservation, Iardasita, Iauna - Craiova, Barzoni Cave, Corcoaia Gorges, Ciucevele Cernei, Closanilor Rock, Stan’s Peak and Tesna Valley), that represent protected areas with the purpose of preserving natural habitats and important species from floristic, faunistic, forest, hydrological, geological, speleological, paleontological, pedological perspectives. Geographically, the park stretches over the Cerna river basin, from its source to the confluence with the Belareca River, overpassing the Godeanu Massif and the Cernei Mountains on the right, and the Valcan and Mehedinti Mountains on the left. In the vicinity of the park, archaeological sites can be found, such as the ones from Zavoi, Voislova and the archaeological site from Baile Herculane, as well as monasteries like Tismana Monastery and Cioclovina de Jos. Domogled-Cerna Valley National Park can be accessed from Drobeta-Turnu Severin, Orsova, Timisoara, Caransebes, Targu Jiu, Baia de Arama, Petrosani, Lupeni.Inside the park, we find Cerna Sat, a few hamlets and at its edge, we find the town of Baile Herculane which, although not included in the area of the park, is surrounded by 95% of its surface.

assertText//div[@id='sectionCollapse2']/pThe history of the settlement is strongly related to the roman castra Drobeta, a strategic point and a place where both land and water roads met and led north and south of the Danube. During ancient Roman times, it became the first urban center in the region and the third in the province of Dacia, after Sarmisegetusa and Apullum. During Hadrian's time in 121, the city was declared a municipality, and in 193, when the population reached 14,000, it was raised to the rank of a colony, which gave citizens of the city and the same rights that Roman people had. In the middle of the 3rd century, Drobeta stretched over 60 hectares and was inhabited by approx. 40,000 inhabitants. The castra was destroyed by barbarians and rebuilt repeatedly in antiquity. In the Middle Ages, the Kingdom of Hungary built the fortress of Severin, which was destroyed by the Ottoman Empire in 1526. An important role in the history of Romania was played by the Banat of Severin, Banatus Zewriniensis, Terra Zeurino or the Land of Severin, which included Caras and a strip of Transylvania, the Land of Hateg, the Land of Amlas and a limitrophe part of Oltenia. The first Ban of Severin, Luca, is mentioned in 1233, during King Andrew II of Hungary. The fortress of Severin has a particular history in that its role and importance were predominantly military, the trade being difficult because of its geographical position and the fact that it was ruled in a permanent succession, by either the kings of Hungary or the princes of Wallachia.On April 23, 1833, the city of Turnu Severin was founded again, when Alexandru Dimitrie Ghica issued the act of the establishment of the city. The original plan of the city was designed by architect Xavier Villacrosse, seconded by engineer Moritz von Ott. Interestingly enough, in 1865, the Romanian inhabitants of Severin were outnumbered by those speaking the German language. Alongside the Austrians and Germans, also Italians, Hungarians, Czechs, Jews, Greeks, Serbs, and Armenians were drawn to the developing city, which, at that time, was the only one to have a naval port and a shipyard. On the 8th of May, 1866 Prince Carol I is arriving at Severin to take over the rule of Romania. Four years later, in 1870, Queen Elizabeth is welcomed in a great manner in the port of Severin, as she prepares to leave the country for the first time. In the interwar period, Turnu Severin becomes one of the first urban centers of Romania. Among the historical monuments that can still be visited in Drobeta, besides the ruins of the Roman castra and Traian's Bridge, are the Roman amphitheater or the Drobeta baths or thermae. Trajan's Bridge - built following the plans of the architect Apolodor of Damascus between 103-105 years, was considered the most daring engineering work of Roman antiquity. The bridge was built on 20 pillars of stone blocks, being 1,135m long, 14m wide and 18 m tall. For the wooden part of the construction, oak trees from 200 hectares of forest were used. The chosen place was downstream of where Drobeta nowadays lies, because the river waters calmed down after leaving the Cazane Gorge, and the riverbed was loaded with enough rocks to provide a stable foundation for sustaining the construction. The bridge linked the Pontes Castra, on the southern shore, to the Drobeta Castra, on the northern shore, and allowed the passage of a marching cavalry legion. At the two ends of the Bridge – Pontes, the southern end and Drobeta, the northern end -, monumental gates were built, as a sign of the greatness and power of the Roman Empire. Drobeta's thermae or public baths were the most complicated thermal installations in Lower Dacia. Today, less than half of the ruins of terms are preserved. They were built by soldiers of the Legion V Macedonica, at the same time as the castra. In addition to the usual facilities of some thermae, such as the fire room (praefurnium), the steam room (laconicum), the bath room (caldarium), the cold water room (frigadarium) and others, on the north side there were reading halls and a space for exercises and sports competitions. The Roman Amphitheater of Drobeta was carved on Trajan's Column. Its ruins were only discovered in 2010, and archaeological work is not over yet. Historians estimate that his could arguably be one of the largest amphitheaters on the territory of Dacia, currently only three of them on Romania’s territory.
